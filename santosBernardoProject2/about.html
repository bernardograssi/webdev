Understanding the Connections Among the Images

Ever since a little kid, I was thirsty for knowledge, trying to understand the universe and the reason why we are here. Throughout my life, I have tried to connect the dots and make sense out of human life, and all the images and topics from this webpage are related to that journey to discovery. Some topics may seem not related to the attempt to understand the universe, but they all played an important role for me and had their own contribution.

Astronomy

Astronomy has always been a core field of interest in my academic endeavours. When I was 10 years old, I tried to make a compilation of all the interesting astronomical facts and theories and put them together in a single book. During my teenage years, that interest grew exponentially, as I began getting in touch with more advanced scientific and philosphical materials, which naturally led me to question what we are doing in this universe, why and how are we here? And of course, to understand that, I had to understand the universe itself first, and astronomy, alongside physics, were the areas I had no other choice but to explore.

This attempt to connect the dots of the puzzle that the meaning of life presents to us would be meaningless without astronomy. To the uncautious observer, astronomy should not be important when a human being is trying to understand its purpose and meaning in life, but for the most careful one, it is clear that astronomy is way more than just observing stars and comets, it is actually a vital key to understand why we are here and how it all came to be.

Greek Mythology

Greek mythology has been a topic I have been interested for a while now. As a kid I used to think that the Greek gods existed for real, but as I grew older and understood that myths are instrisically not true, I understood that although they did not exist in real life, the Greek gods had a strong influence in the conception of Western civilization as we know today.

Besides subtle influences on language, such as by giving names to certain words/expressions like "Herculean task", "aphrodisiac", "morphine", and "trojans" (computer viruses), Greek mythology had a substantial impact in the arts and culture. Sandro Boticelli's The Birth of Venus and Dante Alighieri's frequent references to Greek mythology are two examples of where the art/culture sepctrum is evaded by the influence of Greek mythology.

And of course, even astronomy had suffered from the influence of the Greek, as many constellations and planets carry the names of Greek mythological characters, such as Venus, Andromeda, and Gemini. I always thought this topic was fascinating and represents an important piece of mankind's endeavour to explain the world as it is and how we as species were conceived.

Greek Philosophy

I do not know how a person in the 21st century would be able to go after the meaning of life and understand his purpose in the universe without first going through the teachings of the Greek philosophers. Through the early teachings of Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle, mankind was able to take important steps in the direction of understanding the universe as it is, and how humans are important in it.

Socrates, known as the founder of philosphy, had a meaningful impact in the way that people used to think. Through his dialetics and his personal way of finding out the truth, he presented to mankind the act of philosophizing. Socrates, who was condemned to death in Athens for corrupting the youth of the time, understood that for knowledge to be acquired, it must first be put through judgement and "be cast on fire", and if it survives, it is meaningful. His impact on my personal journey had much to do with the fact that I became a person who would understand how unlikely it is that everything I thought I knew was actually true. Socrates humbled me and thought me to question everything, no matter what.

Plato conveyed and expanded the work of his teacher Socrates, and founded The Academy, which is most likely one of the world's first university and the place where he trained his pupil, Aristotle. Besides having that influence on building the foundations of the Western's way of teaching, he had a massive influence on my thought through his belief on the importance of mathematics in education. It has proven to be essential for understanding the entire universe and has helped me out quite a lot thus far.

From Aristotle, we can learn the basis of physicial sciences as we know today. He was responsible for the first questions about nature, how it works, why it works the way it does, and what is our place in it? He built the foundation of modern science alongside other many thinkers, who were also thirsty for knowledge. Most of the scientifical work in the Middle Ages were based on Aristotelian thought and ideas.

Stoicism

The stoics were for sure the ones who had the deepest impact on myself. Basically, stoicism is a school of Philosophy founded by Zeno of Citium in Athens in the early 3rd century BC. Besides the influence of its founder, the stoic school had on Seneca, Epictetus, and Roman Emperor Marcus Aurelius its prominent spokepersons.

The Stoic school believes in 4 essential values in human life: Courage, Temperance, Justice, and Wisdom. They are konwn for teaching that "virtue" is the only good for human beings, and external things such as wealth, fame, and pleasure are not good or bad in themselves, but have value as "material ofr virtue to act upon". Being virtuous, for the Stoics, had much to do with distinguishing what is in your control and what is not in your control in life. They also believed that the best indication of an individual's philosophy was not what a person said but how a person behaved, and in order to live a good life, one had to understand the rules of the natural order since they thought everything was rooted in nature.

Stoicism played a critical role in making me understanding the world I am in. It was through Stoicism that I became a very down-to-earth person, aware of the externality of all the events of the world, meaning that the events that occur are external to me, other people's feelings and opinions are also external. It thought me that I had to care about what I can control, and nothing else. It made me way more resilient and had me focus on myself, my own development, and my own core feelings.

Human History

It is fairly obvious why human history is important to understand the universe and help understand the meaning of life. I only got really into history when I was around 19-20 years old, as it was the time I realized that for me to understand the present, I need to get in touch with the past and be conscious of what has happened to mankind so far. Well, history is basically a story of the human race, and it tells us a lot of how humans have changed and how the conceptions of the universe have shifted drastically.

My initial interest in history came from my studies of Art History, but it quickly spread out to other topics such as prehistory, Muslim history, Greek history, and, of course, Brazilian history. I could not leave the history of my country behind, because I deeply believe that a man who does not know history is faded to repeat the same mistakes of the past, and I do not want to have Brazil repeat the same mistakes of the past. Ever.

From my studies of history, I was able to understand how the homo sapiens evolved, how it dominated the neanderthals, and how the human life has changed so much in some aspects (such as by technological development) and yet so little in others (we still struggle to understand our meaning in the universe). Besides that, I could get a grasp on how civilizations emerged and developed, how countries were born and how they dominated others through the power of coersion. I understood how governments arose and how they have nothing to do with human development. There is a reason why things are the way they are today, and history teaches us that. But we must not forget that if we want to change the world, we must look back in the past for inspiration.

Rationalism and Existentialism

Philosophy as a whole played a massive role in my path to self-discovery and in my attempt to understand the meaning of life. As aforemnetioned, the Greek philosophers and the stoics were important for me, but I cannot leave behind the other two schools of philosophy that shaped my thought so much.

Firstly, Rationalism. Through the lens of René Descartes and mainly Baruch Spinoza, I managed to see the world through a completely different perspective than I used to. When I firstly got in touch with Spinoza's concept of God, my mind was completely blown away because I realized that what he was saying was exactly what I used to believe, but could not put into words. Deus sive natura, Spinoza's slogan in Latin for "God or Nature", represents the view that God and Nature are interchangeable, so that there is no distinction between the creator and the creation. Besides his influence on my conception of God/Nature, Spinoza, in alignment with the stoics, thought me that a virtuous life is one made out of simplicity, and that a virtuous man is a man who lives according to reason. "The highest activity a human being can attain is learning for understanding, because to understand is to be free."

Now, the existentialists. Albert Camus is my favorite existentialist philosopher, although he did not like to be labeled as "existentialist". It was through his work that I could understand that there is no inherent/objective meaning in life, but subjective meaning. Instead of believing in an objective meaning as Søren Kierkegaard did, Camus believed that each of us have to discover what we are here for. For some, it may be simple and take a few years to find out, whereas for others, it may take a lifetime to figure it out. Through the Myth of Sisyphus, Albert Camus convinced me of the absurdity of life, but also of the fact that we ought to bravely face it every day of our lives.

Søren Kierkegaard has an important play in my life because he, just like Baruch Spinoza, was brave enough to go against the common sense of his days, and had no fear of speaking his truth, no matter how disliked it was. Although I am not a huge fan of his personal philosophy, I do respect his bravery for speaking his mind and for enduring the many problems life brought to him.

Art

Art played an important role in my discovery of the classics. It was through art that I had my curiosity estimulated for history and philosophy. I still remember to this day when I first saw Rafael's masterpiece School of Athens and Michelangelo's David. I remember how amazed I was by the fact that they made works of art so close to perfection such a long time ago. What had me intrigued was the references they had in their works and the incentives behind each work. I became fascinated with the fact that these wonderful artists knew so much about the classic Greco-Roman culture, which led me to study a little bit of that as well.

The period of the Renaissance was for sure the one that grabbed most of my interest in this field, as I believe the most wonderful works of art were produced in this period. It was in the Renaissance that several artistic techniques were developed, such as the chiaroscuro, perspective, foreshortening, and proportion. Amazed by the beauty of the masterpieces produced by Michelangelo, Rafael, Leonardo da Vinci, Rembrandt, and Jacques-Louis David, I wanted to learn more about the things they were painting or sculpting. Looking at their works, I wanted to understand why Michelangelo would depict the Biblical figure David and The Creation of Adamthe way he did, or why Rafael would depict The School of Athens the way he did.

Soccer

Although this is probably the only topic in this page that does not belong to the academic or scientific spectrum, I put playing soccer as one of the most important things that happend to me. To quote Albert Camus, who happened to be a goalkeeper just as I am, "everything I've learned on ethics comes from [playing] soccer". Playing this sport really changed my life and I will explain why.

Since a kid, I used to love the game, always played on the streets of Brazil, and always nurtured an unexplainable love for my hometown club, Atlético Mineiro, depicted in the image. The love I have always felt for this club surpasses the limits of reason. Through the good and bad times, I have always been there supporting the "Galo" (Rooster) as it is known back home. But I never played the game seriously until I was around 15 years of age, which was when I began practicing for real and bagan playing games.

During 2015 and 2016 I played for a club in Minas Gerais called Paraense F.C., where I created strong bonds with players I had never seen before, and if it was not for soccer, I would most likely never meet. That team was a real family and we went through a lot together, always working hard and pushing ourselves to the limit. I still remember when I had my first save in a penalty shootout that led the team to the quarter-finals of the state championship. The feeling was unknown to me. The feeling of beating adversity. The feeling of making your coaches and teammates proud. The feeling of making yourself proud. That day made me realize soccer was way more than just a game, it was part of my DNA.

From playing in U-15 and U-17 championships in 2015-2016 to playing amateur championships in 2017-2018, I took a major step in my career and began playing with "the big boys". Such a period was of tremendous learning for me, on and off the field. From being the 3rd keeper who no one talked cared about to being the unlikely hero of the City Championship Finals in 2017, I learned that limits, like fears, are just an illusion. Soccer taught me I should never get comfortable with myself and my skills, and that I should always strive for more, because there is always something I can improve. It thought me what love is, because I can still remember the passion and love in the eyes of my teammates when we were playing throughout Minas Gerais as the underdogs who no one thought much of. Although it gave me great momments I will never forget, it taught me how to recover from the low moments of life, because it has shown me that no matter how down you are, you can always rise again.

And of course, soccer gave me the opportunity to come to the United States pursue my undergraduate degree, something that was unimaginable for me a few years ago. To soccer I owe much of who I am, and much of who I will become.
